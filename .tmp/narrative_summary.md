## The Physics of Consciousness

This discussion features Sir Roger Penrose explaining his often-misunderstood theories on consciousness. He traces the origin of his ideas to Gödel's incompleteness theorems and argues that consciousness is not a computational process, but rather arises from a specific, non-computable physical process in the brain involving the objective collapse of the wave function.

## Key Quotes

> "I had sort of vaguely heard that Gödel's theorem showed that there were things in mathematics you couldn't prove. I didn't like the idea very much, but then when I heard what it actually said, I found this stunning." - [Roger Penrose]

> "It's not that consciousness causes the collapse of the wave function. My view is almost the opposite. It's that whatever consciousness is, it depends on the collapse of the wave function." - [Roger Penrose]

## Main Content

### The Misunderstood Link Between Consciousness and Gödel's Theorem

[Curt Jaimungal] begins by asking [Roger Penrose] what he is most misunderstood about. `[00:00:00]`](https://youtu.be/92-02TOjntI?t=0s) Penrose replies that it is likely his work on consciousness, a subject he admits is murky and one he was hesitant to get into.

His journey into the topic began unexpectedly during his time as a graduate student in Cambridge. He attended three courses outside his primary field: one on general relativity by **Hermann Bondi** [Austrian-British mathematician, 1919-2005], one on quantum mechanics by **Paul Dirac** [English theoretical physicist, 1902-1984], and a third on mathematical logic by a lecturer named **Steen**. It was in this third course that he encountered **Kurt Gödel's** [logician and mathematician, 1906-1978] incompleteness theorems, which he found "absolutely stunning."

Penrose explains the core of Gödel's theorem: for any consistent formal system of rules, one can construct a statement that essentially says, "I am not provable by these rules." If the statement were false, it would be provable, and therefore true—a contradiction. Thus, the statement must be true. The profound implication for Penrose was that a human mind can see the truth of this statement, not by using the formal rules, but by understanding the system from the outside. This, he argues, is a non-computational act. Our belief in the truth of the statement comes from our trust in the rules, but the statement itself cannot be derived from within those rules. This suggested to him that human understanding and consciousness possess a quality that transcends formal computation.

### Consciousness as a Consequence, Not a Cause, of Collapse

Penrose contrasts his view with one often attributed to **Eugene Wigner** [Hungarian-American theoretical physicist, 1902-1995], which posits that a conscious observer is required to collapse the quantum wave function. `[00:06:43]`](https://youtu.be/92-02TOjntI?t=403s) Penrose recalls discussing this with Wigner, who he says was less dogmatic about the idea than is commonly believed.

To illustrate his disagreement, Penrose offers a thought experiment: imagine a distant, lifeless, Earth-like planet. Its weather system would exist in a quantum superposition of all possible states. A space probe photographs this superposition and sends the image back to Earth. The moment a conscious being on Earth views the image, the weather on the distant planet retroactively resolves into a single state. Penrose finds this scenario nonsensical.

His own view is nearly the opposite: consciousness does not *cause* the collapse, but rather *depends* on it. He sees the collapse of the wave function (objective reduction) as the only physical process not obviously computable. While current physics models this collapse with randomness, Penrose argues this is merely an approximation of a more complex, non-random, non-computable process. He believes there is a deeper story to be explored regarding the puzzling nature of time in relation to wave function collapse, which could be relevant to conscious perception.

### The Role of Brain Structures and Pyramidal Cells

Penrose notes that he is not an expert in neurobiology and cannot comment on the specific chemicals involved. However, he was impressed by the work of his collaborator, **Stuart Hameroff** [anesthesiologist and consciousness researcher]. `[00:12:33]`](https://youtu.be/92-02TOjntI?t=753s) He points out the difference between the cerebrum and the cerebellum. The cerebellum is organized logically, much like a computer, and is completely unconscious. The cerebrum, associated with consciousness, has a much stranger, less organized structure.

Hameroff's work highlights the importance of **pyramidal cells**, a type of neuron found in the cerebrum but not in the cerebellum. Penrose finds it "quite plausible" that these specific cells, which are unique to the conscious part of the brain, could be the relevant structures for the physical processes underpinning consciousness.

### Objective Reduction and the Lifetime of a Superposition

[Curt Jaimungal] questions whether a computer, as a physical system, could be conscious if the collapse of the wave function is a physical process. `[00:14:07]`](https://youtu.be/92-02TOjntI?t=847s) Penrose clarifies that the process would need to be specifically harnessed. His theory of objective reduction posits that collapse is not caused by an observer, but happens spontaneously when a system's superposition becomes "too big."

He describes a calculation he developed to determine the lifetime of a superposition. By calculating the gravitational self-energy required to separate an object (like a cup) from a superimposed copy of itself, one can find the lifetime of that state. For a macroscopic object, this lifetime is instantaneous. For a molecule, it could be much longer. This lifetime calculation was independently discovered about two years prior by **Lajos Diósi** [Hungarian physicist], and Penrose sometimes refers to it as the "Diósi lifetime."

However, Penrose's scheme differs from Diósi's. Diósi's model predicted a constant, spontaneous heating effect from collapsing superpositions. An experiment conducted in a mountain laboratory a few years ago failed to detect this heating, refuting that aspect of the theory. Penrose states his model does not involve this heating, which he considers a "nuisance" that would create cosmological problems, such as with the observed temperatures of neutron stars.

### Quantum vs. Classical Reality and Retrocausality

To avoid the heating problem, Penrose's theory requires something more curious: a form of retrocausality, where effects can seem to precede their causes. `[00:16:31]`](https://youtu.be/92-02TOjntI?t=991s) To make sense of this, he distinguishes between two kinds of reality:

1.  **Classical Reality**: This applies to objects like a cup. You can ask it its properties (shape, size), and it has definite answers.
2.  **Quantum Reality**: This applies to a particle's spin. You cannot ask a particle "which way are you spinning?" and get a direct answer. You must suggest a direction. If you are correct, the measurement will confirm it with 100% certainty, satisfying **Albert Einstein's** [German-born theoretical physicist, 1879-1955] criterion for an "element of reality." However, if you are wrong, the outcome is probabilistic. The particle does not have a definite state independent of measurement.

This quantum reality exhibits strange causal behavior that can appear to go backward in time. Penrose asserts that this does not lead to true causal anomalies (like predicting the past) because it is confined to the quantum realm. He mentions he is considering writing a more complete paper to explain this complex interaction between quantum and classical realities.
